What is Money?

Everyone feels the pain. Few understand what's causing it.

A Word From the Author

In 2020, my wife and I were expecting our first child. We owned a home. We were making more money combined than ever before. But for some reason, we just couldn't get ahead.

We'd been in our house since 2018 and hadn't built any real savings — nothing to prepare for a baby and everything that comes with one. I couldn't wrap my mind around why we were "just getting by."

So I checked the spending. We weren't going on trips. We ate out only a few nights a month — I actually like to cook at home. Amazon packages showed up from time to time, but nothing outrageous. I assumed it had to be indiscriminate spending. Nope. Checked the grocery bill — ouch. Checked the electric bill. Checked how much gas we were burning through.

It wasn't a lavish lifestyle draining our bank accounts. It was just living. And every year, living cost more. Not because we'd changed our habits — because the money itself was changing. Becoming worth less.

That realization started me down a path: why is life just so costly? And that trail led me to the question I had never thought to ask before — "What is money?"

The answer to that question led me to Bitcoin. But first, I needed to understand why the ruler we were using to measure the cost of living kept shrinking. Let's find out.

01

Something Doesn't Add Up

Think About This

Your parents bought a house on a single income. You need two incomes just to rent an apartment. Your grandparents raised a family of five on a factory worker's salary. You have a college degree and still live paycheck to paycheck. Gas was less than $1.00/gallon when your dad learned to drive. Now it's over $3.00. A dozen eggs cost less than a dollar in 2000 — in early 2023, it hit $4.82.

You're told the economy is growing. So why does it feel like you're falling behind?

Most people describe this feeling the same way: "Prices keep going up." That's what it looks like. But that's not what's actually happening.

Prices aren't going up. Your dollar is going down.

The Core Insight

When everything costs more, the problem isn't the goods — it's the unit of measurement. Imagine your ruler shrinks by 1% every year for 111 years. Every table you measure looks taller, even though nothing has actually changed. That's what's happened to the dollar.

02

The Gold Lens: A Better Ruler

Imagine This

You're using a ruler to measure a table. Every year, someone sneaks in and shaves a quarter-inch off your ruler. The table hasn't changed — but every time you measure, it looks bigger. That's what's happening to the dollar. The economy is the table. The dollar is the ruler. And the Federal Reserve has been shaving the ruler for 111 years.

Here's where inflation becomes undeniable: compare prices in dollars to prices in gold.

Oil priced in dollars exploded 8,005%. Oil priced in gold actually fell 36%. A barrel of oil is cheaper in gold today than it was in 1913. Housing? It went up 30% in gold over 111 years — driven by population growth, land scarcity, and zoning restrictions. But compare that 30% to the 16,380% dollar increase. Almost all of that sticker shock is the dollar losing value, not the house gaining it. In real terms — measured in gold — the world is more productive and most things should cost about the same or less. They don't in dollars, because the dollar itself is worth less.

Why Gold Is the Better Ruler

Gold's supply grows by about 1.5–2% per year through mining — roughly matching the growth rate of the global economy. You can't print gold. You can't create 40% more of it overnight. For thousands of years, gold served as money precisely because its supply was hard to manipulate. It was the ultimate honest ruler.

The dollar, on the other hand, has no limit. The Federal Reserve can create as much as it wants, whenever it wants, for whatever reason it wants. And it does.

Two Schools of Thought

Keynesian economists argue moderate inflation is healthy. It encourages spending, prevents hoarding, and keeps the economy moving. From this view, the dollar losing value is a feature, not a bug — a tool for managing the economy.

Austrian economists say that narrative confuses cause and effect. They argue that inflation is an expansion of the money supply that transfers wealth — from savers to borrowers, from ordinary people to those closest to the money printer. From this view, the dollar losing 97% of its value is theft in slow motion.

This course will present both perspectives. But we'll always check them against the data — because facts don't care about economic theories.

The 2% Lie

The Federal Reserve targets "2% annual inflation" and calls it responsible policy. Sound reasonable? Even at a clean 2%, compounding over 111 years would destroy 89% of your purchasing power. But the actual loss is 97% — because real inflation has averaged closer to 3.2% per year, not 2%. The Fed's own data proves the target was never actually met. Meanwhile, gold — inflating at roughly 1.5–2% as the actual economy grows — has kept its purchasing power almost perfectly intact. Which system looks healthy when you see the long-term picture?

03

What Is Money?

Before we can understand why the dollar is failing, we need to understand what money is and how it evolved.

The Problem Money Solves

Early humans bartered — I trade you a chicken for your basket. But this creates a problem called the double coincidence of wants. You have chickens. I have baskets. But what if you don't want baskets, and I don't want chickens? Trade breaks down.

Money solved this by becoming a universal intermediary. I sell my baskets for money, you sell your chickens for money, and now I can buy your chickens anytime. Money is the lubricant that makes the economy work.

Gold Wins

Thousands of years ago, civilizations independently discovered that gold worked best. It's scarce, durable, divisible, portable, and widely accepted. Civilizations from Egypt to Rome to China converged on gold as the ideal money — not because anyone mandated it, but because it worked better than everything else.

The Real Pattern

When multiple independent cultures choose the same solution, that's not an accident. It's evidence that the solution is right. Gold wasn't imposed by decree — it was discovered through trial and error to be the best money that naturally emerged. For thousands of years, gold was the money.

The Break: 1971

In 1971, President Nixon ended the gold standard. The dollar was no longer backed by a fixed amount of gold. Instead, it became fiat currency — money by government decree, with nothing backing it but the government's promise to accept it for taxes.

This is where the two economic schools diverge sharply. Keynesian economists see this as necessary evolution — freeing governments to respond flexibly to crises. Austrian economists see it as the moment governments gave themselves permission to debase their citizens' savings without limit.

What happened to the dollar after 1971? The numbers are staggering. In 1971, gold was fixed at $35 per ounce. Today it trades above $4,400. That's not gold "going up" — that's the dollar collapsing against real money. The dollar has lost over 99% of its value measured in gold since the tether was cut. And the acceleration is the key: it took 58 years (1913–1971) for the dollar to lose about 41% of its gold value — from $20.67 to $35 per ounce. It took just 55 more years to lose virtually all of what remained.

Before 1971, the gold standard acted as a leash — governments could only print as many dollars as they had gold to back. After 1971, the leash came off. The data from Section 2 — oil and housing getting cheaper in gold but exploding in dollars — that acceleration happened almost entirely after this moment.

Money Trends Toward One

Throughout history, money doesn't stay diverse. It consolidates. People abandon weak money for strong money. The dollar won because it was stronger than other fiat currencies. Bitcoin is emerging because it's stronger than all existing forms of money combined — as a store of value, at least. We'll explore why in the next lesson.

04

The Inflation Machine Unleashed

Everything described above has been happening gradually for decades. But in 2020, the pace went from a steady drip to a firehose.

When COVID-19 shut down the economy, the US government responded with trillions in stimulus spending. The Federal Reserve created new money on an unprecedented scale. The M2 money supply — a measure of all dollars in circulation including savings and money market accounts — tells the story:

It took 200+ years (from 1800 through early 2020) to put ~$15.4 trillion into circulation. Then the money supply exploded — growing by roughly 40% in just two years, jumping to over $21 trillion by 2022 and remaining elevated through 2024. Over $6 trillion in new dollars were injected into the system.

What Happened Next

When you create 40% more money in two years, two things happen: (1) people have more dollars, so they bid up prices, and (2) each dollar is worth less. The official inflation rate peaked at 9.1% in 2022. But real-world experience — at the grocery store, at the gas pump, on your rent check — felt far worse.

The CPI Doesn't Tell the Full Story

Since 1971, the government has measured inflation using the Consumer Price Index (CPI). But the CPI methodology has been quietly adjusted many times — hedonic adjustments, substitution effects, geometric weighting — each revision making the number look lower. If you used the pre-1980s methodology on today's data, official inflation would often read 3–5% higher than reported.

Trueflation — a decentralized, real-time tracker monitoring over 15 million actual retail and housing prices — provides a reality check. Since 1971, the official CPI says cumulative inflation is roughly 700%. But measured against gold, the dollar has lost over 99% of its value in the same period — a 12,500% devaluation. That gap tells you everything about how well the government's own yardstick measures the damage. The CPI is measured with the very ruler that's shrinking.

Both Schools Agree on What Happened, Disagree on Whether It's Good

Keynesian economists call this "fiscal stimulus" — necessary intervention to prevent economic collapse. Austrian economists call it "monetary debasement" — a hidden tax on everyone who holds dollars. Both agree on the facts: trillions of new dollars were created from nothing, driving up prices. They just disagree on whether that's a cure or a poison.

$10,000 in 2010 Is Worth $6,800 Today

If you saved $10,000 in 2010 and did nothing with it, you lost $3,200 in purchasing power by 2024 — not from bad spending, not from a failed investment, but simply from holding the currency. That's not an investment loss. That's taxation without legislation. That's the theft of your future.

Unless you hold a form of money they can't print.

A Keynesian vs Austrian Perspective

A Keynesian economist would say "Look, wages went up too. 859% is growth." An Austrian economist would ask "Into what prices?" The wage growth is largely an illusion — you're getting more dollars that buy less of what matters.

"Inflation is taxation without legislation."
— Milton Friedman, Nobel Prize-winning Economist

Think about who gets hurt: people on fixed incomes (retirees), people with savings (the responsible), people earning wages without negotiating power (workers). Who benefits? Those with debt (mortgages, business loans) see their obligations shrink in real terms. Those closest to new money creation (big banks, governments, large corporations) get to spend it before prices adjust. Those at the back of the line (ordinary workers, savers) watch their purchasing power evaporate.

The Real Cost

When necessities cost more and wages lag, families are forced to make impossible choices: Do we buy quality groceries or pay rent? Do we save for emergencies or pay for childcare? Do we invest in education or keep food on the table? Inflation transforms from an economic concept into a moral crisis.

06

Money With Scarcity Built In

Gold solved the inflation problem for thousands of years. But gold has practical limitations — it's heavy, hard to verify, hard to divide for small transactions. That's why we eventually switched to paper money backed by gold. Then, in 1971, we dropped the backing entirely.

What if there was a form of money that had gold's scarcity — the thing that kept prices stable — but was digital, portable, and divisible? What if you could send it in seconds? What if no one could print more, no matter what emergency came along?

The Bottom Line

Bitcoin is the best of gold and fiat, without the drawbacks of either. It has gold's scarcity and finality — the properties that kept prices stable for millennia. It has fiat's convenience and speed. But unlike gold, you can send it anywhere in seconds. And unlike the dollar, no one can print more. The measuring stick can't be shaved.

Common Questions

Q: I keep hearing inflation is "only 2–3%." Why doesn't that match what I see at the store?

A: Because the government's number — the Consumer Price Index — is an average that gets adjusted in ways that consistently make it look lower. It uses tricks like "substitution" (if steak gets too expensive, they assume you switch to chicken and call that stable pricing) and "hedonic adjustments" (your new phone has a better camera, so they say it got cheaper even if the price went up). The number you see on the news and the number you feel in your wallet are two very different things.

Q: Wait — does this mean my savings account is actually losing money?

A: Yes. If your savings account pays 0.5% interest but real inflation runs 3–5% per year, you're losing 2.5–4.5% of your purchasing power every single year. You still see the same number in your account — or slightly more — but what that money buys shrinks. It's like having a slow leak in your tire. You don't notice it day to day, but over a few years, you're running on flat.

Q: If gold holds its value so well, why don't we just use gold?

A: Gold is excellent at storing value — that's exactly what the data in this lesson shows. But it has real limitations as everyday money. It's heavy, hard to divide for small purchases, slow to verify, and impossible to send digitally. That's why we moved to paper backed by gold — and eventually dropped the backing altogether. The question becomes: is there something with gold's scarcity but without its drawbacks? That's exactly where the next lesson picks up.

Q: What can I actually do about this?

A: That's what this entire course is built to answer. Step one is what you just did — understanding the problem. You can't fix what you can't see. Step two is learning about tools that exist outside the system of endless money printing. The rest of this course walks you through the most important one — and how to use it to protect yourself and your family.

Key Takeaways

  • Prices aren't going up — the dollar is going down. Priced in gold, oil is actually 36% cheaper than in 1913. Housing rose only 30% in gold over 111 years — compare that to the 16,380% increase in dollars. The dollar lost 97% of its CPI purchasing power in the same period. The problem isn't the goods — it's the measuring stick.
  • Gold maintained purchasing power because its supply grows slowly, roughly matching economic growth. The dollar failed as a store of value because its supply expands without limit — roughly 3.1% per year on average, and far more during crises like 2020.
  • Money's function is to solve the barter problem by serving as medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account. For thousands of years, gold was the best money because it was scarce and stable. The dollar was good when backed by gold. It became a poor choice once the backing was removed.
  • The Keynesian view says moderate inflation is healthy. The Austrian view says it's wealth transfer from savers to borrowers. The data suggests the Austrians have the stronger case: 97% of your savings' value gone in 111 years isn't "moderate."
  • The money supply grew ~40% in just two years starting in 2020. Over $6 trillion in new dollars were created, causing 9.1% official inflation and worse real-world inflation for the necessities people actually buy. The people who can least afford it paid the highest price.
  • Bitcoin combines gold's scarcity with digital convenience. No one can expand its supply. No committee can debase it. In the next lesson, we'll learn exactly what Bitcoin is and how it achieves what gold and the dollar cannot.

Glossary

Terms are clickable in the HTML version of this lesson. This section is a full reference.

Austrian Economics
A school of economic thought emphasizing free markets, sound money, and minimal government intervention. Austrian economists view inflation as an expansion of the money supply that transfers wealth from savers to those closest to the money printer.
Bitcoin
A decentralized digital currency with a fixed supply of 21 million coins. It combines the scarcity of gold with the convenience of digital money. No central authority controls it.
Cantillon Effect
The observation that newly created money doesn't enter the economy evenly — it benefits those who receive it first (banks, governments, large institutions) while diluting the purchasing power of those who receive it last (ordinary workers and savers).
Consumer Price Index (CPI)
The government's primary measure of inflation, tracking price changes in a basket of goods and services. Critics argue its methodology has been adjusted over decades to understate actual inflation experienced by consumers.
Deflation
A decrease in the general price level — meaning your money buys more over time. This happens naturally when productivity increases faster than the money supply.
Double Coincidence of Wants
The problem in barter where both parties must want exactly what the other has at the same time and place. Money solves this by acting as a universal intermediary.
Fiat Currency
Government-issued money that isn't backed by a physical commodity like gold. Its value comes from government decree and public trust. The US dollar, euro, and yen are all fiat currencies.
Federal Reserve
The central bank of the United States, created in 1913. It controls the money supply and sets interest rates. Its ability to create new dollars is the mechanism through which inflation occurs.
Gold
A precious metal that served as the world's primary money for thousands of years due to its scarcity, durability, and difficulty to counterfeit. The US dollar was backed by gold until 1971.
Inflation
The expansion of the money supply, which results in each unit of currency buying less over time. Commonly described as "rising prices," but more accurately understood as the currency losing purchasing power.
Keynesian Economics
A school of economic thought advocating for government intervention, central bank management of the money supply, and moderate inflation as tools for economic stability. Named after economist John Maynard Keynes.
M2 Money Supply
A measure of the total amount of money in circulation, including cash, checking deposits, savings accounts, and money market accounts. Used as a key indicator of how much money exists in the economy.
Medium of Exchange
One of the three functions of money. Something widely accepted in trade so people don't have to barter directly. The dollar serves this role today; Bitcoin is increasingly doing so.
Money
A tool that solves the barter problem by serving as a medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account. Good money is scarce, durable, divisible, portable, and widely accepted.
Money Supply
The total amount of money in circulation in an economy. When the money supply grows faster than the production of goods and services, inflation results.
Purchasing Power
The amount of goods or services a unit of currency can buy. When inflation occurs, purchasing power decreases — each dollar buys less than it used to.
Store of Value
One of the three functions of money. Something that maintains its purchasing power over time. Gold is a strong store of value; fiat currencies are weak stores of value due to inflation.
Trueflation
A decentralized, real-time inflation index that tracks over 15 million items using actual retail, housing, and economic data. Provides an alternative measurement to government-reported CPI.
Unit of Account
One of the three functions of money. A standard measurement for pricing goods and services. The dollar is the dominant unit of account in the US.
