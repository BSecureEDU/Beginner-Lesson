What is Money?

Everyone feels the pain. Few understand what's causing it.

01

Something Doesn't Add Up

Think About This

Your parents bought a house on a single income. You need two incomes just to rent an apartment. Your grandparents raised a family of five on a factory worker's salary. You have a college degree and still live paycheck to paycheck. Gas was $1.00/gallon when your dad learned to drive. Now it's over $3.00. A dozen eggs used to cost less than a dollar — in early 2023, it hit $4.82.

You're told the economy is growing. So why does it feel like you're falling behind?

Most people describe this feeling the same way: "Prices keep going up." That's what it looks like. But that's not what's actually happening.

Prices aren't going up. Your dollar is going down.

Every year, each dollar you hold buys a little bit less. The coffee. The gas. The rent. It all costs "more" — but only because the dollars you're paying with are worth less than they used to be. This isn't bad luck, and it isn't greed. It's the predictable result of a system that creates new money faster than the economy creates new goods.

More dollars chasing not that much more stuff. That's the formula. And it's been running for decades.

Key Insight

When someone says "prices are rising," flip it in your mind: the dollar is losing purchasing power. It's not that things are getting more expensive — it's that your money is getting weaker.

02

Money: The Tool That Changed Everything

Before money, humans had to barter — trade goods directly with each other. If you raised chickens and needed shoes, you had to find a shoemaker who wanted chickens, at the exact time you both had something to trade. Economists call this the double coincidence of wants, and it's a terrible way to build an economy.

Without money, every trade requires both parties to want exactly what the other has — at the same time and place.

Money solves this problem. It's a tool — an intermediary good that everyone agrees to accept, so you can sell your chickens to anyone, hold the value, and buy shoes whenever you want. Good money needs to serve three roles:

Throughout history, people have used shells, beads, salt, cattle, and dozens of other things as money. But over time, money always trends toward one — the one thing that best holds value, is hardest to produce more of, and is most widely accepted.

For thousands of years, that was gold. Gold is rare, it doesn't rust or corrode, it's divisible, and you can't just print more of it. Gold earned its role as money because of its physical properties — not because any government declared it.

A Real Pattern

Every civilization that discovered gold independently — from the Egyptians to the Aztecs to the Chinese — came to the same conclusion: this is money. Not because they talked to each other. Because gold has the right properties. Money isn't chosen by decree. It's discovered through competition.

Eventually, carrying gold became impractical. Governments created paper currencies backed by gold — a receipt that said "this note can be exchanged for gold at the bank." That system worked, until governments decided they wanted to spend more than their gold could back.

In 1971, President Nixon officially ended the gold standard. The dollar was no longer redeemable for gold. The money you carry today is fiat currency — it has value because the government says it does, and because everyone agrees to accept it. There is nothing backing it except trust.

Key Concept

Money trends toward one. Historically that was gold. Currently it's the US dollar. But the dollar's reign depends on one thing: people's continued trust that it will hold value. What happens when that trust erodes?

That 97% figure isn't an accident of history — notice the date. 1913 is when the Federal Reserve was created, giving a central bank the power to expand the money supply. The dollar has been on a one-way trip downward ever since.

Homes went up 1,730%. Incomes only went up 717%. That gap isn't supply and demand. Supply and demand causes short-term fluctuations — a drought drives up egg prices for a season, a housing boom inflates one city's market. But when everything gets more expensive, consistently, for 50+ years? That's not any single market. That's the money itself.

This Is Not Supply and Demand

When gas goes up one summer because of a refinery shutdown — that's supply and demand. When gas, and eggs, and milk, and housing, and healthcare, and tuition all go up relentlessly for decades? There's only one thing they all have in common: they're all priced in the same dollars. The problem isn't the goods. The problem is the unit of measurement.

This is inflation — not "prices going up," but the money supply expanding faster than the supply of goods and services. More dollars chasing not that much more stuff. Every new dollar created dilutes every dollar already in your pocket.

"Inflation is taxation without legislation."
— Milton Friedman, Nobel Prize-winning economist
04

When Innovation Beats the Printer

If money printing makes everything more expensive, why are some things getting cheaper? Great question — and the answer actually proves the point.

A Real Example

In 1997, a 32-inch standard-definition TV cost around $1,000. Today, you can buy a 65-inch 4K smart TV — four times the screen size, incomparably better picture, built-in streaming — for under $400. That's a better product at a fraction of the price.

Here's what happened: technology and manufacturing got so much more efficient that the cost of production fell faster than the money printer could dilute the dollar. Innovation outran inflation.

You see this in computing, electronics, solar panels, and communication. The cost of a gigabyte of storage was $300,000 in 1980. Today it's about $0.015. That's not because the dollar got stronger — it's because human productivity and innovation are that powerful.

The Hidden Truth

In a world without money printing, most things should be getting cheaper over time — not more expensive. As technology improves and production becomes more efficient, prices should naturally fall. The fact that they don't — that your grocery bill keeps climbing despite incredible advances in farming, logistics, and technology — tells you exactly how aggressively the money supply is expanding. Inflation is stealing the gains that innovation should be giving you.

"The natural state of a free market is that prices fall to the marginal cost of production."
— Jeff Booth, The Price of Tomorrow

Think about what that means. In a free market with honest money, competition and innovation should drive prices down toward what it actually costs to make something. That's progress working in your favor. But when the money supply is constantly expanding, it masks that progress — you never see the savings. Prices stay flat or climb, and you assume that's normal. It's not. You're being robbed of a future where life gets cheaper every year.

The things getting cheaper are the things technology can disrupt faster than the government can print. When the things you can't live without keep costing more, that's not progress. That's a broken monetary system.

And what happened next? Exactly what you'd expect. In June 2022, inflation hit 9.1% — the highest in 40 years. Groceries surged. Rent spiked. Gas hit $5/gallon in many states. The dollars you'd been saving were suddenly worth dramatically less.

This Isn't Ancient History

This happened to you. If you had $10,000 in a savings account in January 2020, its purchasing power dropped to roughly $8,100 by 2024 — even if the number in your account didn't change. You didn't spend it. You didn't lose it. It was taken from you through inflation. Every new dollar printed diluted every dollar you'd already earned.

The Core Problem

The US dollar has no cap on supply. There is no rule, no law, and no mechanism that prevents the government from creating more whenever it decides to. The decision to devalue your savings is made by a small group of people you didn't elect, in meetings you weren't invited to, and there is nothing you can do to stop it — unless you hold a form of money they can't print.

06

The Best Money Ever Created

So here's the situation: gold has great properties as money — it's scarce, durable, divisible, and no one can print more of it. But it's heavy, hard to transport, hard to verify, and nearly impossible to use for everyday purchases. That's why we switched to paper money in the first place.

And fiat currency (like the dollar) is easy to transact with — you can send it electronically, pay bills online, carry it in your pocket. But it inflates endlessly, can be frozen or seized, and you don't actually control it — your bank does.

What if there was a form of money that combined the best properties of both — without the drawbacks of either?

That's Bitcoin.

Gold's Strengths — Without Gold's Weaknesses

🥇 What Gold Gets Right

  • Scarce — can't be manufactured
  • Divisible — can be split into smaller units
  • Final settlement — once transferred, it's done
  • No counterparty risk — you hold it, you own it

🥇 What Gold Gets Wrong

  • Heavy and hard to transport
  • Difficult to verify without expertise
  • Hard to divide for small purchases
  • Requires physical security (vaults, guards)

Bitcoin inherits all of gold's monetary strengths — it's capped at 21 million coins forever, it's divisible down to 100 millionths (called satoshis), and when you send it, settlement is final. But because it's digital, you can send it across the world in minutes, verify it instantly, and secure it with a password instead of a vault.

Fiat's Strengths — Without Fiat's Weaknesses

💵 What Fiat Gets Right

  • Easy to move and transact
  • Widely accepted
  • Divisible into small amounts (cents)
  • Digital transfers are fast

💵 What Fiat Gets Wrong

  • No supply cap — inflates endlessly
  • Can be frozen, seized, or censored
  • You don't control it — banks and governments do
  • No final settlement — transactions can be reversed

Bitcoin takes everything convenient about digital money — instant transfers, easy subdivision, global reach — but removes the fatal flaw. No one can print more of it. No bank can freeze your account. No government can inflate it away. You hold it. You control it.

The Bottom Line

Bitcoin is the best of gold and fiat, without the drawbacks of either. It has gold's scarcity and finality. It has fiat's convenience and speed. But unlike gold, you can send it in seconds. And unlike the dollar, no one can print more. In the coming lessons, we'll learn how Bitcoin achieves each of these properties — and why they can't be faked or taken away.

Common Questions

Q: Isn't some inflation normal and healthy?

A: Central banks target 2% inflation, calling it a sign of a "healthy economy." But 2% per year means your money loses half its value every 36 years. That's not healthy for savers — it's a policy choice that punishes people who try to save and rewards those who borrow. In a world with advancing technology, prices should naturally fall over time, not rise.

Q: Don't wages go up to match inflation?

A: Sometimes — but never evenly, and rarely fast enough. Since 1970, housing has risen ~1,730% while median income has risen ~717%. Wages tend to lag behind cost-of-living increases, especially for people on fixed incomes, retirees, and lower-wage workers. Inflation hits the people who can least afford it the hardest.

Q: If the government just stopped printing money, would prices go down?

A: The dollars already in circulation don't disappear. But if the money supply remained fixed while productivity improved, prices would gradually fall — you'd be able to buy more with each dollar over time. This is called deflation, and it's what naturally happens in a sound monetary system. Your money would actually gain value for being saved.

Q: Why should I care about this? I'm not an economist.

A: You don't need to be. You already feel it — at the gas pump, at the grocery store, when you look at home prices. Understanding why it's happening is the first step to protecting yourself from it. That's what the rest of this course is about.

Key Takeaways

  • Prices aren't going up — the dollar is going down. When the money supply expands faster than the economy produces goods, each dollar buys less. That's inflation.
  • Money is a tool that solves the barter problem. Good money must serve as a medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account. Money always trends toward one — historically gold, currently the dollar.
  • Long-term price increases across all goods aren't supply and demand — they're monetary inflation. Short-term fluctuations are market forces. Decades-long trends across every category point to one cause: more dollars being printed.
  • The money supply grew by ~40% in just a few years. Over $6 trillion in new dollars were created starting in 2020, pushing M2 from $15.4 trillion to over $21 trillion — and it hasn't come back down. The consequences hit immediately: 9.1% inflation, surging costs, and eroded savings.
  • Innovation should make life cheaper. In many sectors, technology is driving prices down. But inflation steals those gains in the things you need most: housing, food, healthcare, and education.
  • Bitcoin combines the best properties of gold and fiat currency without the weaknesses of either. Scarce like gold. Convenient like the dollar. Controlled by no one but you.

Glossary

Terms are clickable in the HTML version of this lesson. This section is a full reference.

Bitcoin
A decentralized digital currency with a fixed supply of 21 million coins. It combines the scarcity of gold with the convenience of digital money, and no central authority controls it.
Deflation
A decrease in the general price level — meaning your money buys more over time. This happens naturally when productivity increases faster than the money supply.
Double Coincidence of Wants
The problem in barter where both parties must want exactly what the other has at the same time and place. Money solves this by acting as a universal intermediary.
Fiat Currency
Government-issued money that isn't backed by a physical commodity like gold. Its value comes from government decree and public trust. The US dollar, euro, and yen are all fiat currencies.
Federal Reserve
The central bank of the United States, created in 1913. It controls the money supply and sets interest rates. Its ability to create new dollars is the mechanism through which inflation occurs.
Final Settlement
A payment that is fully and irreversibly complete. Once settled, neither party can reverse or undo it. Gold and Bitcoin offer final settlement; bank transfers and credit cards do not.
Gold
A precious metal that served as the world's primary money for thousands of years due to its scarcity, durability, and difficulty to counterfeit. The US dollar was backed by gold until 1971.
Inflation
The expansion of the money supply, which results in each unit of currency buying less over time. Commonly described as "rising prices," but more accurately understood as the currency losing purchasing power.
M2 Money Supply
A measure of the total amount of money in circulation, including cash, checking deposits, savings accounts, and money market accounts. Used as a key indicator of how much money exists in the economy.
Medium of Exchange
One of the three functions of money. Something widely accepted in trade so people don't have to barter directly. The dollar serves this role today; Bitcoin is increasingly doing so.
Money
A tool that solves the barter problem by serving as a medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account. Good money is scarce, durable, divisible, portable, and widely accepted.
Money Supply
The total amount of money in circulation in an economy. When the money supply grows faster than the production of goods and services, inflation results.
Purchasing Power
The amount of goods or services a unit of currency can buy. When inflation occurs, purchasing power decreases — each dollar buys less than it used to.
Satoshis
The smallest unit of Bitcoin, equal to 0.00000001 BTC (one hundred-millionth of a bitcoin). Named after Bitcoin's pseudonymous creator, Satoshi Nakamoto.
Scarcity
A limited supply that cannot be easily increased. Gold is scarce because it's difficult to mine. Bitcoin is scarce because its supply is mathematically capped at 21 million coins.
Store of Value
One of the three functions of money. Something that maintains its purchasing power over time. Gold is a strong store of value; fiat currencies are weak stores of value due to inflation.
Unit of Account
One of the three functions of money. A standard measurement for pricing goods and services. The dollar is the dominant unit of account in the US.
